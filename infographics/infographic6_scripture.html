THE SCRIPTURE EVIDENCE

Every significant Bible passage relating to the Sabbath — from Creation through the Apostles — with what Saturday-keepers and Sunday-keepers each argue, and what the text actually says.

THE CREATION ACCOUNT — Genesis
The Sabbath exists before the Law of Moses, before Israel, before any covenant — rooted in creation itself
Genesis 2:2–3
"And on the seventh day God ended His work which He had made; and He rested on the seventh day from all His work which He had made. And God blessed the seventh day, and sanctified it: because that in it He had rested from all His work which God created and made."
Three actions: God rested, blessed, and sanctified the seventh day. This is before Adam sinned, before Abraham, before Moses, before Israel — the Sabbath is embedded in the structure of creation itself. Saturday-keepers argue this makes the Sabbath universal and eternal, not a Jewish institution.
Key Saturday Evidence
Genesis 2:3 — "Sanctified"
The Hebrew word "qadash" — sanctified, set apart as holy. This is the first use of "holy" in the entire Bible. It is applied to a day, not a place or person.
The significance: holiness is first assigned to time — specifically to the seventh day. Saturday-keepers argue no subsequent text ever removes this holiness or transfers it to another day.
First Use of "Holy" in Scripture
Exodus 16:23–30 — Before Sinai
"Tomorrow is the rest of the holy Sabbath unto the LORD... the LORD hath given you the Sabbath... See, for that the LORD hath given you the Sabbath, therefore He giveth you on the sixth day the bread of two days."
The Sabbath is introduced to Israel in Exodus 16 — before the Ten Commandments at Sinai in Exodus 20. God gives them double manna on Friday and none on Saturday, establishing the pattern. This proves the Sabbath was known before the Law was formally given.
Pre-Sinai Evidence
Isaiah 66:22–23
"For as the new heavens and the new earth, which I will make, shall remain before me... And it shall come to pass, that from one new moon to another, and from one sabbath to another, shall all flesh come to worship before me, saith the LORD."
A prophetic passage describing worship in the New Creation — after Christ's return. It specifically names the Sabbath as the day of universal worship in eternity. Saturday-keepers cite this as the most powerful evidence that the Sabbath is eternal, not abolished.
Eternal Sabbath Prophecy

THE FOURTH COMMANDMENT — Exodus & Deuteronomy
The most detailed of all Ten Commandments — the only one beginning with "Remember"
Exodus 20:8–11 — The Fourth Commandment
"Remember the sabbath day, to keep it holy. Six days shalt thou labour, and do all thy work: But the seventh day is the sabbath of the LORD thy God: in it thou shalt not do any work, thou, nor thy son, nor thy daughter, thy manservant, nor thy maidservant, nor thy cattle, nor thy stranger that is within thy gates: For in six days the LORD made heaven and earth, the sea, and all that in them is, and rested the seventh day: wherefore the LORD blessed the sabbath day, and hallowed it."
The most detailed of all Ten Commandments — 94 words compared to "Thou shalt not kill" (6 words). Begins with "Remember" — implying it was already known. Points back to Creation as its foundation. Includes strangers/foreigners — not limited to Israel. Saturday-keepers ask: if Sunday replaced Saturday, why is there no commandment to "Remember the first day"?
The Fourth Commandment — Longest of the Ten
Deuteronomy 5:12–15 — Second Record
"Keep the sabbath day to sanctify it, as the LORD thy God hath commanded thee... And remember that thou wast a servant in the land of Egypt, and that the LORD thy God brought thee out thence."
The second giving of the commandments adds a second reason for the Sabbath: remembrance of deliverance from slavery. Two foundations — Creation and Liberation — are woven into Saturday's sacred character.
Two Foundations: Creation + Liberation
Exodus 31:16–17
"Wherefore the children of Israel shall keep the sabbath, to observe the sabbath throughout their generations, for a perpetual covenant. It is a sign between me and the children of Israel for ever."
Sunday-keepers often cite this verse to argue the Sabbath was "a sign" specifically for Israel — and therefore not binding on Gentile Christians. Saturday-keepers counter that the covenant applies to all who are grafted into Israel through faith (Romans 11).
Disputed: Israel-only or universal?

JESUS AND THE SABBATH — The Gospels
Jesus kept the Sabbath, taught in synagogues on Saturday, and declared himself "Lord of the Sabbath" — but never abolished it
Luke 4:16
"And he came to Nazareth, where he had been brought up: and, as his custom was, he went into the synagogue on the sabbath day, and stood up for to read."
"As his custom was" — Jesus habitually, regularly kept the Sabbath. This is not a one-time act but a lifelong practice described as his personal custom.
Jesus' Personal Custom
Mark 2:27–28
"The sabbath was made for man, and not man for the sabbath: Therefore the Son of man is Lord also of the sabbath."
Saturday-keepers: "made for man" — for all humanity, not just Jews. Sunday-keepers: Jesus being "Lord of the Sabbath" means he has authority to change it. Saturday-keepers respond: a lord owns and preserves, not abolishes.
Disputed: "Made for man" — all humanity?
Matthew 5:17–18
"Think not that I am come to destroy the law, or the prophets: I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil. For verily I say unto you, Till heaven and earth pass, one jot or one tittle shall in no wise pass from the law, till all be fulfilled."
Saturday-keepers: "till heaven and earth pass" — heaven and earth still exist, therefore the law (including the Fourth Commandment) is still in force. Sunday-keepers: "fulfil" means Jesus completed the law's requirements, making ceremonial laws no longer binding. The debate is whether the Sabbath is "moral" or "ceremonial."
Most Debated Passage
Matthew 24:20
"But pray ye that your flight be not in winter, neither on the sabbath day."
Jesus instructs his disciples — speaking prophetically about the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 AD and beyond — to pray that their future flight would not fall on the Sabbath. Written for a future audience still observing Saturday. Saturday-keepers: would Jesus instruct prayer about a day he intended to abolish?
Jesus Assumes Future Saturday Keeping
Luke 23:54–56 — After the Crucifixion
"And that day was the preparation, and the sabbath drew on... And they returned, and prepared spices and ointments; and rested the sabbath day according to the commandment."
The women who were closest to Jesus — after his death — "rested the sabbath day according to the commandment." The disciples kept Saturday even on the weekend of the resurrection. The resurrection itself occurred after the Sabbath rest was complete.
Disciples Rest on Saturday After Crucifixion
John 20:19 — "First Day" Appearances
"Then the same day at evening, being the first day of the week, when the doors were shut where the disciples were assembled for fear of the Jews..."
Sunday-keepers cite this as the first "Sunday worship" — Jesus appearing to disciples on the first day. Saturday-keepers: the disciples were hiding in fear, not worshipping. The text gives no instruction to make this a weekly observance. It is a single appearance, not a command to change the Sabbath.
Disputed: Fear-gathering or Sunday worship?

THE ACTS OF THE APOSTLES — The Early Church
Paul consistently worships on Saturday — including with Gentiles — throughout his entire missionary career
Acts 13:14, 42–44
"They came to Antioch in Pisidia, and went into the synagogue on the sabbath day... And when the Jews were gone out of the synagogue, the Gentiles besought that these words might be preached to them the next sabbath... And the next sabbath day came almost the whole city together to hear the word of God."
Paul preaches to both Jews and Gentiles on Saturday. The Gentiles specifically ask to hear more on "the next sabbath" — not on Sunday. "Almost the whole city" gathers the following Saturday. If Paul intended to change the Sabbath to Sunday, this was the moment to say so. He does not.
Gentiles Invited to Next Saturday
Acts 16:13
"And on the sabbath we went out of the city by a river side, where prayer was wont to be made; and we sat down, and spake unto the women which resorted thither."
In Philippi — a Roman colony with no synagogue — Paul and his companions still observe the Sabbath by going to a riverside prayer meeting on Saturday. There is no synagogue to "reach Jews" as an excuse; this is pure Sabbath observance by the apostolic team.
Paul Keeps Saturday Outside Synagogue
Acts 17:2
"And Paul, as his manner was, went in unto them, and three sabbath days reasoned with them out of the scriptures."
"As his manner was" — exactly the same phrase used of Jesus in Luke 4:16. Paul habitually, as his personal custom, observed the Sabbath. This is in Thessalonica — a predominantly Gentile city. Paul's manner was Saturday worship, not Sunday.
Paul's "Manner" = Saturday, Like Jesus
Acts 18:4, 11
"And he reasoned in the synagogue every sabbath, and persuaded the Jews and the Greeks... And he continued there a year and six months, teaching the word of God among them."
In Corinth, Paul teaches every Saturday for 18 months. He teaches both Jews AND Greeks (Gentiles) on Saturday. 78 consecutive Saturdays of apostolic teaching — with no mention of Sunday worship replacing it.
78 Saturdays of Teaching — Jews & Gentiles
Acts 20:7 — "First Day of the Week"
"And upon the first day of the week, when the disciples came together to break bread, Paul preached unto them, ready to depart on the morrow; and continued his speech until midnight."
The most frequently cited Sunday worship verse. Sunday-keepers: the church met on the first day. Saturday-keepers: this was a one-time farewell meeting before Paul's departure, not a regular Sunday service. The meeting went to midnight — consistent with Saturday night by Jewish reckoning (the first day begins at sunset Saturday).
Most Cited Sunday Verse — Disputed
Acts 20:7 — Saturday Night Interpretation
In Jewish reckoning, the day begins at sunset. "The first day of the week" beginning at Saturday sunset means this meeting was Saturday night — after the Sabbath ended. Paul then traveled on Sunday (the morrow/next morning), consistent with no Sunday rest obligation.
Saturday-keepers: the meeting was Saturday night, making it a post-Sabbath fellowship, not a Sunday worship service. This interpretation is consistent with Paul departing the very next morning.
Saturday Night by Jewish Reckoning

PAUL'S LETTERS — The Most Debated Passages
The passages Sunday-keepers rely on most heavily — and the Saturday-keeper responses
SATURDAY-KEEPER READING
Colossians 2:16–17
"Let no man therefore judge you in meat, or in drink, or in respect of an holyday, or of the new moon, or of the sabbath days: Which are a shadow of things to come; but the body is of Christ."
The "sabbath days" here are annual ceremonial sabbaths (feast days), not the weekly Saturday Sabbath. The Greek word is plural "sabbatōn" referring to annual holy days. The weekly Sabbath in the commandment is singular. Paul is defending believers from being judged for how they observe ceremonial feast days.
Romans 14:5
"One man esteemeth one day above another: another esteemeth every day alike. Let every man be fully persuaded in his own mind."
Context is about Jewish fast days and feast days — not the weekly Sabbath. The passage is about Christian freedom regarding ceremonial observances, not about changing the commandment Sabbath. No Saturday-keeping church in Rome is being rebuked.
VS
SUNDAY-KEEPER READING
Colossians 2:16–17
The weekly Sabbath is included in "sabbath days" — Paul is saying the entire Sabbath system (weekly and annual) was a shadow pointing to Christ. Now that the reality (Christ) has come, the shadow (Sabbath) is no longer binding. Christians are not to be judged by others for not keeping it.
This is the primary Sunday-keeper argument for Sabbath abolition — that Colossians 2:16 cancels the Fourth Commandment by making the weekly Sabbath ceremonial rather than moral.
Romans 14:5
Paul teaches that all days are equal to the mature Christian — no day is inherently more sacred than another. This includes Saturday. Christians are free to worship on any day; the Old Testament Sabbath is not specifically binding on New Testament believers.
Sunday-keepers read this as Paul liberating Christians from the Sabbath commandment, replacing it with freedom of worship on any day.
1 Corinthians 16:2 — "First Day" Offering
"Upon the first day of the week let every one of you lay by him in store, as God hath prospered him, that there be no gatherings when I come."
Sunday-keepers: Paul instructs first-day giving — implying a Sunday gathering. Saturday-keepers: "lay by him in store" means at home — private saving for a future collection, not a church offering at a Sunday service. No mention of Sunday worship here — Paul is trying to avoid having to collect money when he arrives.
Home saving vs. Sunday collection
Hebrews 4:9–10
"There remaineth therefore a rest to the people of God. For he that is entered into his rest, he also hath ceased from his own works, as God did from his."
The Greek word used is "sabbatismos" — literally "a Sabbath-keeping." Saturday-keepers: this explicitly says a Sabbath-keeping remains for God's people. Sunday-keepers: the "rest" is spiritual/eternal rest in Christ, not a weekly Saturday observance. The word "sabbatismos" appears nowhere else in the New Testament.
"Sabbatismos" — A Sabbath-keeping Remains

REVELATION — "The Lord's Day" and the Eternal Sabbath
The single most debated verse in the Sunday-vs-Saturday discussion
Revelation 1:10 — "The Lord's Day"
"I was in the Spirit on the Lord's day, and heard behind me a great voice, as of a trumpet."
Sunday-keeper reading: "The Lord's Day" = Sunday, the day of resurrection. By John's time (c. 95 AD) Sunday had become known as "the Lord's Day" among Christians. This is the earliest clear biblical reference to Sunday as a distinct Christian observance.

Saturday-keeper reading: "The Lord's Day" in the Old Testament is "the Day of the LORD" — a prophetic concept of God's day of judgment (Isaiah 13:6, Joel 1:15, Amos 5:18). John, who was steeped in Hebrew prophetic tradition, used it in this sense — not to designate Sunday worship. Also: Jesus declared himself "Lord of the Sabbath" — making Saturday "the Lord's Day."
The Most Debated Verse in the Debate
Isaiah 58:13 — God Names the Day
"If thou turn away thy foot from the sabbath, from doing thy pleasure on my holy day; and call the sabbath a delight, the holy of the LORD, honourable..."
God calls the Sabbath "my holy day" — using "my" as a possessive. If the day that belongs to God is "the Lord's Day," Saturday-keepers argue this is the original scriptural claim on that title — predating Revelation by centuries. In Exodus 20:10 God calls it "the sabbath of the LORD thy God" — again using LORD as possessive of Saturday.
God's Own Name for His Day = Saturday

THE CENTRAL QUESTION
  • Was the Sabbath "moral" or "ceremonial"? If moral (like "do not murder"), it is permanent. If ceremonial (like sacrifices), Christ fulfilled and ended it.
  • Is Colossians 2:16 about annual feasts or the weekly Sabbath? The answer changes everything about Paul's argument.
  • What does "Lord's Day" mean in Revelation 1:10? Sunday resurrection day — or the Old Testament "Day of the LORD"?
  • What does Jesus mean by "fulfil" in Matthew 5:17? Complete and end — or fill to the fullest meaning?
  • If the Sabbath changed, where is the command? The Catholic Mirror's challenge: show us the verse that transfers the Sabbath to Sunday.
WHAT THE SCRIPTURE RECORD SHOWS
The Saturday Sabbath appears explicitly and positively in scripture from Genesis 2 through Isaiah 66, through the Fourth Commandment, through every Gospel account of Jesus' practice, through every recorded missionary journey of Paul. The New Testament contains no verse commanding Christians to worship on Sunday, no verse abolishing the Fourth Commandment, and no verse transferring the Sabbath from the seventh day to the first.

What the New Testament contains are disputed passages — John 20:19, Acts 20:7, 1 Corinthians 16:2, Colossians 2:16, Revelation 1:10 — none of which unambiguously commands Sunday worship. The Catholic Church's own scholarship acknowledges this plainly. The historical record shows Sunday was established by state law (321 AD) and church decree (364 AD) — not by any text of scripture.

The most powerful single verse in the entire debate may be Hebrews 4:9 — "There remaineth therefore a sabbatismos to the people of God" — which uses the unique Greek word for "a Sabbath-keeping" and says it remains.
