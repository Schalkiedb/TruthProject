Saturday Persecution Across Continents — Before America Ever Existed

52–1599 AD · Kerala, India

The St. Thomas Christians of Kerala trace their origin to the Apostle Thomas arriving in India in 52 AD. They worshipped on Saturday and were described by later Portuguese missionaries as "Sabbath-keeping Judaizers." In 1599, Archbishop Menezes of Goa convened the Synod of Diamper and ordered the burning of all Nasrani texts — the Gospel of Thomas, the Acts of Thomas, and the ancient Aramaic Peshitta Bible — to erase their "Jewishness." Saturday worship was forcibly replaced with the Latin rite. 1,547 years of Saturday observance ended not by scripture but by fire.

1273–1450 AD · Ethiopia, Africa

Monk Ewostatewos (1273–1352) insisted on Saturday Sabbath, citing the "Canons of the Apostles." Persecuted by the Ethiopian hierarchy under pressure from Alexandria's Coptic Patriarch, he fled c. 1337 through Sudan, Egypt, Jerusalem, Cyprus, dying in Armenian exile. His followers — the "Sabbatarians" — retreated to remote northern Ethiopia. In 1400, Emperor Dawit I summoned them to court and had them arrested. They were finally vindicated in 1450 at the Council of Dabra Metmaq, when the Emperor challenged the bishops to show where in scripture — the Octateuch, the Prophets, the Gospels, or the Apostles — any basis existed for abolishing Saturday. They could find none. Both Saturdays and Sundays were declared sacred. A 113-year persecution of Saturday-keepers in Africa, ended by that single question.

563–597 AD · Iona, Scotland

St. Columba sailed to Iona in 563 AD and built the school that evangelised all of Scotland and much of Northern England. The Celtic Church observed Saturday as the Sabbath. On his deathbed, June 9, 597 AD, Columba told his disciple Diermit: "This day is called the Sabbath, that is, the rest day, and such will it truly be to me." To suppress Celtic Saturday observance, Rome deployed a forged "Letter of Jesus" — falsely claimed discovered on the altar of St. Peter's — threatening divine punishment for working on Sunday. Legends of bread bleeding when cut on Sunday, and iron grown fast to the hand of a man who cleaned his plough, replaced scripture as the enforcement tool.

c. 1070–1115 AD · Scotland & Wales

As late as c. 1070, the Scots "worked on Sunday but kept Saturday in a sabbatical manner" (Andrew Lang, History of Scotland, Vol. I). Queen Margaret convened a three-day royal council and, citing Pope Gregory, demanded the people cease Saturday rest and venerate Sunday. Within a generation, Saturday was suppressed across Scotland. Wales held out until 1115 AD. The Celtic church — which had kept Saturday for five centuries — was finally conquered not by scripture but by royal authority and papal pressure. The same template: state power enforcing religious practice, minority faith left unprotected, theological justification arriving after the political fact.

Pattern Across All Four: Exile · Arrest · Book-burning · Forgery · Royal enforcement · Scripture absent · Minority faith left unprotected — 52 AD, 1337 AD, 886 AD, 1070 AD, 1885 AD, 2026 AD

I. The Making of the Crisis — How Sunday Laws Were Weaponised

1838 → 1879 → 1885 · The Law is Already There

Sunday laws — also called Blue Laws — had existed across the American states since colonial times, inherited from English Puritan tradition. For most of the 19th century, they were largely ignored and unenforced. Farmers worked their fields on Sunday. Merchants traded. Courts sat. No one was arrested.

That changed in the late 1870s. The National Reform Association (NRA) — a Christian lobbying group founded in 1864 with the declared goal of inserting "Jesus Christ" into the US Constitution — began a systematic campaign to revive and strengthen Sunday laws. They were joined by the Woman's Christian Temperance Union (WCTU), the American Sabbath Union, and dozens of Protestant denominations. Their aim was simple: a Christian America, enforced by law.

When the NRA succeeded in getting state Sunday laws re-enacted or strengthened — particularly in the South — law officers made a critical decision: they ignored all other violations (hunting, fishing, casual commerce, Sunday trading by their own church members) and focused their prosecutions exclusively on Seventh-day Adventists — the one group whose conscience forced them to work on Sundays because they had already rested on Saturday.

"In both Henry and Rhea counties, Tennessee, the chain-gang had fallen into disuse because it was found to be unprofitable, and it was revived specially for the punishment of Seventh-day Adventists." — The American Sentinel, September 5, 1895
"Persons who are members of any religious society, who observe as Sabbath any other day of the week than the Christian Sabbath or Sunday, shall not be subject to the penalty of this act, so that they observe one day in seven..." — Arkansas Revised Statutes of 1838 (the exemption that protected Saturday-keepers)
In 1884, Arkansas legislators — ostensibly trying to close Jewish-owned saloons on Sunday — repealed this exemption. Once it was gone, law officers ignored the saloonkeepers entirely and began arresting Adventist farmers. — The Great Controversy, Ellen G. White / Spectrum Magazine legal analysis
"The neighbors formed an enforcement league, pledging to 'prosecute each and every violation of the Sunday law of our state that may come under our observation.' Despite this threat, people in that area who were not Seventh-day Adventists continued hunting and fishing and doing farm work... but enforcement league members ignored them and focused on the Adventists." — Adventist Encyclopedia, King, Robert M. (1856–1891)

Washington County, Arkansas · Spring 1885 · Scoles v. State

Rev. James W. Scoles — Minister Arrested

First of 21+ Arkansas prosecutions · Arrested for painting their church building on Sunday

Five Adventists including their minister were arrested in spring 1885 after a neighbour testified to the grand jury that he "knew of Seventh-day Adventists who work on Sundays." The crime: painting the exterior of their church building on Sunday, May 3, 1885. The exemption protecting Saturday-keepers had been stripped from Arkansas law the previous year. The case reached the Arkansas Supreme Court: Scoles v. State (1886) — first of 21+ Arkansas Sunday law cases, nearly all Adventists.

The American Sentinel publicised the cases so widely that freedom was eventually restored to the imprisoned Adventists — but the persecution continued for years

Obion County, Tennessee · 1889–1891 · United States Supreme Court · The Defining Case

Robert M. King — The Poor Farmer Who Almost Reached the Supreme Court

Died Nov 10, 1891 Wife & 6 children left behind
His Story

Robert King converted to Adventism in 1884. His Methodist neighbours — fearing his example would "confuse their children as to which day really was the Sabbath" — demanded he either quit Saturday observance or leave the community he had lived in his whole life.

He replied: "This is a free country and I am allowed to worship God in accordance with my understanding of the Scriptures." When threatened with prosecution he said he was a poor man and could not afford to give up 1/6 of his work week.

The Arrest

On June 23, 1889, King went out to cultivate his cornfield — far from the road. The corn was so tall as to "nearly hide him from sight." A Sunday school superintendent rode to the back of his lot and waited patiently until King emerged from between the rows of corn.

He was arrested and fined $12.85. He paid. He was then re-indicted on the grounds that his Sunday plowing constituted a "public nuisance." Non-Adventist neighbours who hunted and fished on Sundays were never prosecuted.

The Federal Court & the End

A federal court admitted Tennessee had punished King unjustly and that the Sunday law was "impractical and unjust." The SDA Church petitioned to take the case to the United States Supreme Court — which would have created landmark constitutional precedent on religious liberty.

On November 10, 1891, Robert King died of a heart attack. His case was dropped from the Supreme Court docket. When Tennessee saw this, authorities "stepped up the persecution" — interpreting the removal as a green light. He left a wife and six children.

"The Sabbath before he died he said he had been thinking how God's people have been persecuted in the past, and he felt that God had especially blessed him during the week, and that his faith was stronger than ever before." — Lizzie J. Abonlien, who visited King in prison, Adventist Review & Sabbath Herald, December 18, 1891

III. The Chain Gang — July 18, 1892, Paris, Tennessee

The Day Christians Were Marched Through the Streets in Chains for Keeping the Sabbath
The Five Farmers of Henry County, Tennessee · May–July 1892

The Henry County attorney general — proceeding on the assumption that "Seventh-day Adventists were automatically Sunday law violators" — requested the names of five SDA church members. The five were all farmers. They were indicted for "nuisance" even though four of them lived so far from any road that their work was unlikely to be observed "unless people went out of their way to note it."

Six prosecution witnesses testified that they had not been disturbed by the defendants' Sunday labour. No witness gave the name of any person who was disturbed. Evidence of the following "crimes" was presented:

  • W.S. Lowry: Seen cutting firewood and loading wood on a wagon on Sunday
  • J.H. Dortch: Seen ploughing and hoeing on Sunday
  • Three others: Various "usual farm work" on their own land, far from public view

All five were convicted. They declined to employ counsel and appeared in their own defence. They lay in jail from June 3 to July 18, 1892 — 44 days — before three of them were marched to the chain gang.

"July 18, in the year of our Lord 1892, witnessed a sight that revives the memories of the religious persecutions of the Dark Ages. At Paris, Tennessee, four Christian men had been lying in jail since June 3, 1892, for the crime (?) of following their common avocations on Sunday, by working on the farm, plowing, hoeing. After having lain in jail forty-four days, three were marched through the streets in company with some colored criminals, and put to work shoveling on the common highway." — National Religious Liberty Association pamphlet, "In the Chain-Gang for Conscience' Sake," 1892
The Jewish Man in Butte, Montana — August 1895

A Hebrew man was arrested in Butte, Montana on August 3, 1895 for selling goods without a licence. Being too poor to pay his fine, he was put in the chain gang to work. Being an orthodox Hebrew, when Saturday came he told the authorities he could not work as it was the Sabbath.

"He was put into a dungeon and water pumped in so that he could not sit down... The mayor said that the American people kept Sunday, and it did not matter whether a man kept the seventh day or not, if he was in the chain-gang and refused to work Saturday, he would have to take the consequences."— American State Papers, 1896 Report

IV. The Blair Sunday-Rest Bill — A National Sunday Law Nearly Passed the US Senate (1888)

Senate Bill 2983 · May 21, 1888 · Senator Henry W. Blair, Republican, New Hampshire
What the Bill Proposed

Senate Bill 2983 would have:

  • Banned "secular work, labor, or business to the disturbance of others" throughout the United States on the first day of the week (Sunday)
  • Banned all mail transport on Sundays — including letters of necessity or mercy
  • Banned interstate commerce and rail transport on Sundays
  • Banned military musters, drills, and parades on Sundays
  • Prohibited "play, game, or amusement, or recreation to the disturbance of others" on Sundays
  • Penalties: fines of $10 to $1,000 for non-observance
The Coalition Behind It
  • National Reform Association — the primary lobbying body. Had already spent 24 years pushing to declare America a Christian nation in the Constitution
  • Woman's Christian Temperance Union (WCTU) — under Josephine C. Bateham, added a "Sabbath Observance Department" to collect petition signatures
  • American Sabbath Union
  • Knights of Labor (partially co-opted by Rev. Wilbur F. Crafts)
  • Cardinal James Gibbons (Catholic, Baltimore) — endorsed it, to the shock of many Protestants
  • Methodist, Presbyterian, Baptist, Lutheran denominations — clergy testified en masse at the Senate hearing
Alonzo T. Jones Before the Senate — December 13, 1888

The American Sentinel's co-editor, representing the SDA Church, appeared before Senator Blair's Committee on Education and Labor. The SDA Church collected 260,000 opposing signatures — presented in a stack three feet high, bound in patriotic red, white, and blue. The head of the stack barely appeared above the representative's desk.

"There never was a Sunday law that was not antichristian, and there never can be one that will not be antichristian... Any government which attempts it sets itself against the word of Christ, and is therefore antichristian."— Alonzo T. Jones, testimony before the US Senate, Dec. 13, 1888
How It Was Defeated
  • Blair tried to force the bill to the Senate floor with a discharge petition on the final day of the congressional session — March 1889
  • The opposition of one senator — all that was required — blocked the maneuver
  • Blair rewrote the bill, removing "the more obvious religious implications," and re-introduced it on December 9, 1889
  • The second version also failed. The lesson learned: "the stronger the religious rationale, the stronger the constitutional arguments available to opponents"
  • The SDA Church founded the National Religious Liberty Association in July 1889 — still operating today as the International Religious Liberty Association
  • The petition count was contested: E.J. Waggoner estimated the real total was only one-eighth of the claimed 14 million signatures

1886 · Oakland, California

The American Sentinel

Launched January 1886 from Pacific Press at 50 cents a year. Eight tightly packed pages — no illustrations. Co-editors: Alonzo T. Jones and E.J. Waggoner. Directly descended from the Sabbath Sentinel which had publicised the Arkansas cases.

Its relentless documentation of Sunday law prosecutions brought national newspaper coverage from the Chicago Tribune, New York World, Atlanta Constitution, and Brooklyn Daily Eagle. Public exposure ended the worst of the prosecutions in Arkansas.

Became Liberty Magazine — still published today for legislators and judges

The International Tract Society organised state-by-state canvassing, asking members to sign and collect opposing signatures to the Blair Bill. By March 1889 they had 260,000 names — presented in a stack three feet high, bound in patriotic red, white, and blue.

When a House page delivered the stack to Representative O'Donnell's desk, his head barely appeared above it. The visual was so striking it was reported in Adventist papers as "quite interesting."
